7:30 – 8:30 am
Breakfast in Xi'an
Food
 Start the day with a proper Xi'an breakfast — try rou jia mo (braised pork in a flatbread, often called a Chinese burger) and a bowl of hu la tang (spicy pepper soup). Both are street food staples sold at stalls near the Bell Tower area. The area around the South Gate and Bell Tower has excellent morning street food. Eat well — it's a long and active day before your next proper meal.
8:30 – 9:30 am
Transfer to Terracotta Army
Scenic
 Take a Didi or pre-booked car approximately 40 minutes east of the city centre to the Emperor Qinshihuang Mausoleum Site Museum. A private car is preferable for comfort and flexibility on the return. Avoid the public bus on Day 5 — it stops frequently and adds unnecessary time. A private car or Didi costs around 80–100 RMB each way and is much more efficient.
9:30 am – 1:00 pm
Terracotta Army
Historical
Discovered accidentally by farmers in 1974, the Terracotta Army is one of the greatest archaeological finds of the 20th century. Over 8,000 life-sized warriors, horses, and chariots were buried with Emperor Qin Shi Huang around 210 BC to guard him in the afterlife — each soldier with a unique face. The site fundamentally changed our understanding of ancient Chinese civilisation and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
 Visit all three pits in order — Pit 1 is the largest and most dramatic, Pit 2 has the best individual warrior detail, Pit 3 is the command headquarters. Watch the on-site archaeologists working in real time. The bronze chariot exhibition at the far end of the complex is often overlooked but exceptional. Hire an official on-site guide (~150 RMB) — the stories and context transform the experience. Arriving at 9:30am means the initial morning rush has spread out across the pits. Allow 3–3.5 hours minimum. Book tickets online in advance.
1:00 – 2:00 pm
Lunch near the museum
Food
 Eat at one of the local restaurants in the village just outside the museum's east exit — hand-pulled noodles, cold skin noodles (liang pi), and braised dishes. Heartier and cheaper than anything inside the complex. Avoid the overpriced canteen inside the museum. The village restaurants a 5-minute walk east of the exit gate are far better value and quality.
2:00 – 2:45 pm
Return to Xi'an city
Scenic
 Didi or private car back to the city centre, dropping you at the South Gate (Yongning Gate) of the City Wall — the main entry point for wall visitors and bike rentals. Ask the driver to drop you directly at the South Gate entrance, not the nearby car park — it saves a 10-minute walk with tired legs.
3:00 – 5:30 pm
Xi'an City Wall
Historical
Originally built in the Tang dynasty (618–907 AD) and massively expanded under the Ming dynasty in 1370, Xi'an's city wall is the most complete ancient city wall surviving in China. At 13.7 km in circumference, 12 metres high, and up to 18 metres wide at the top, it once defended one of the most powerful capitals in the medieval world. Walking or cycling its perimeter gives a rare sense of the city's ancient scale.
Option A — Bike the full perimeter
Full 13.7 km circuit by bike
Rent a single bike at the South Gate (around 45 RMB/hr) and cycle the entire perimeter — approximately 1.5 hours at a relaxed pace. The wall is wide enough to cycle comfortably and the views shift constantly between ancient battlements on one side and modern Xi'an on the other. Return the bike at the South Gate where you started.
Best for: the full immersive experience. Timing the ride to finish around 5:30pm puts you on the wall during golden hour — the light on the ancient brick is stunning.
Option B — Walk a section on foot
South Gate to East Gate on foot (~3.5 km)
Enter at the South Gate and walk east along the wall to the East Gate (Changle Gate) — about 45–60 minutes at a leisurely pace. This stretch has the best-preserved watchtowers and the most open views. Take the stairs down at the East Gate and Didi to the Bell Tower for the evening.
Best for: a more relaxed afternoon after the Terracotta Army. Leaves more time and energy for the Bell Tower evening. Ideal if feet are already tired from the museum.
5:30 – 7:00 pm
Freshen up & dinner
Food
 Head to the restaurants around the Bell Tower and Drum Tower square for dinner. Try De Fa Chang for classic Xi'an dumplings (jiaozi banquet), or Lao Sun Jia for authentic mutton paomo — bread broken into a broth, a Xi'an speciality unlike anything in Beijing. Both restaurants are within walking distance of the towers and have been serving Xi'an cuisine for over 100 years. Book a table or arrive by 5:30pm to avoid the dinner queue.
7:00 – 9:00 pm
Bell & Drum Tower light show
Culture
Nightlife
 The Bell Tower (built 1384) and Drum Tower (built 1380) are dramatically floodlit at night, creating one of Xi'an's most iconic evening scenes. Both towers have traditional music performances inside — the Bell Tower features melodic bell-ringing ceremonies, the Drum Tower has thundering ensemble drumming shows running every 30 minutes. The illuminated square between the two is a wonderful place to sit and take it all in. Visit the Drum Tower first for the percussion shows (more dramatic), then the Bell Tower. Combined ticket available. The square outside is free to enjoy — local families and couples gather here every evening. Shows run until around 9pm.
9:00 pm onwards
Evening wind-down
Nightlife
 The streets around the Bell Tower have a handful of good bars and tea houses for a quiet end to the evening. Alternatively, take a short walk north to the edge of the Muslim Quarter for late-night street snacks — the stalls stay open until midnight and are less crowded than during the day. Keep energy in reserve — Day 6 starts with the Great Mosque in the morning and the full Muslim Quarter experience, which is best enjoyed fresh rather than exhausted.
