5:30 – 7:00 am
Tiananmen Square
Historical
The symbolic heart of China for over 600 years — emperors entered the Forbidden City through this gate, and Mao proclaimed the People's Republic here in 1949. At 440,000 sqm, it is one of the largest public squares in the world.
 Watch the flag-raising ceremony at sunrise. Stroll the square and take in the scale of the Gate of Heavenly Peace. Book a free reservation 1 day ahead. Bring passport. Security checks can be slow — arrive early.
7:00 – 8:30 am
Breakfast
Food
 Grab breakfast near Wangfujing or at a local jianbing (savoury crepe) street cart close to the square. Good chance to rest your feet before the palace. The Forbidden City involves a lot of walking.
8:30 am – 12:00 pm
Forbidden City
Historical
Home to 24 emperors across the Ming and Qing dynasties (1420–1912), this is the world's largest and best-preserved palace complex. For nearly 500 years, no ordinary citizen could enter — death was the penalty. Now a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
 Enter via the Meridian Gate (Wumen). Walk the full central axis, explore the eastern and western wings, and visit the Clock Exhibition Hall if time allows. Book tickets online at least 1 week in advance — they sell out. Closed on Mondays. Audio guide is worth it. Wear comfortable shoes.
12:00 – 12:45 pm
Jingshan Park
Historical
Built in the 15th century from earth excavated during the Forbidden City's construction, this artificial hill served as the northern boundary of the imperial city. The last Ming emperor, Chongzhen, famously took his own life here in 1644 as rebels stormed Beijing.
 Exit the Forbidden City through the north gate and cross the road directly into Jingshan Park. Climb 10 minutes to the Wanchun Pavilion at the summit for the best aerial view in Beijing — the entire golden roofscape of the Forbidden City laid out below you, framed by the city skyline. Entry is only 2 RMB — essentially free. Takes 45 minutes total. Go straight to the top before stopping anywhere else. Early afternoon light is ideal for photos looking south over the palace.
1:00 – 2:00 pm
Lunch — Qianmen area
Food
 Head south 15 min on foot or a short Didi to Qianmen. Try Dadong or Quanjude for Peking Duck, or grab lighter bites around Dashilar. This is the perfect spot to eat — right between the palace area and your evening stroll along Qianmen Street.
2:00 – 5:30 pm
Qianmen Street
Evening walk
 Stroll the restored Qing-dynasty boulevard. Watch the vintage tram. Browse the traditional shopfronts and century-old brand stores. Less crowded in the afternoon than at midday. The street runs directly south from the Qianmen Gate.
5:30 – 8:00 pm
Dashilar & hutongs
Evening walk
 Branch west into Dashilar Street — visit Ruifuxiang silk shop (est. 1893) and Tongrentang pharmacy (est. 1669). Then explore Yangmeizhuxie Hutong for indie cafes and art studios. Wander freely — the best discoveries are down the side alleys. The area lights up beautifully at dusk.
8:00 – 9:30 pm
Dinner
Food
 Option 1: Peking Duck at Quanjude (est. 1864) or Dadong — the definitive Beijing meal, served with pancakes, scallions, and plum sauce. Option 2: Dine at one of Dashilar's historic restaurants. Option 3: Wangfujing Snack Street for a lively street food finale. Peking Duck restaurants get busy — book ahead. Dadong is more modern; Quanjude is the classic old-school experience.
9:30 pm onwards
Shichahai Bar Street
Nightlife
 Head to Shichahai lake for lakeside cocktails at one of the many bars lining the water's edge. The illuminated hutong rooftops and willow trees reflecting on the lake make for a magical end to the day. About 15 min by Didi from Dashilar. Houhai Bar Street (the south bank) is the liveliest stretch. Arrive before 10pm for a good table with lake views.
